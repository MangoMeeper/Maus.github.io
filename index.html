Staus

Level 3: Folks in towns that you've been questing in recognize you and will generally have a good disposition toward you. Shopkeepers may give you a discount on items.
Level 4:Folks in a larger area have heard of you or and generally have a good disposition towards you. Strangers will come up to you and ask for help in quests. Nobles may hire you as knights or warriors on dangerous missions. Shopkeepers often give you small discounts or may gift you items.
Level 5: Most everyone has heard of you, mouse or not. Nobles seek you out for special missions and will even let you lead a group of their soldiers. Songs and stories are starting to be written about you. Property and rare items are given to you as gifts from rulers and shop keepers.

Encumbrance

If the maus can't hold it in their inventory, they aren't moving it. You need hirelings or an obvious cart/wagon to move additional items.

Hirelings

A pair of helping paws might give you the edge you’ll need to survive. Throughout the maus kingdoms you’ll come across other maus who are willing to explore the vast unknown world with you and your party.
In a maus settlement, you can spend a day asking around for maus looking for work. The size of the settlement determines what types of hireling are available. Smaller settlements may not have skilled maus looking for work.
Make a WIL save or pay 20p. If successful (or 20p is paid), roll the Number for the type of hireling you are interested in employing to determine how many are looking for work.
Typical hirelings will have:
d6 hp, STR 2d6, DEX 2d6, WIL 2d6
Hirelings have 6 inventory slots: two paw, two body, two pack.

Hireling Morale

When a hireling or warband is placed in a stressful situation , made to go without pay or food, or asked to do something more dangerous than what they signed on for, they must make a WIL save or flee.
Especially well-paid or loyal hirelings may make this save with Advantage.

Hireling Advancement

Hirelings and warbands can improve in the same way that your player maus do.
• Hirelings who receive a share of treasure gain 1 EXP per pip they are given above their daily wage.
• Warbands who receive share of treasure will gain 1 EXP per 10 pips they are given above their weekly wage.
Hirelings and warbands advance at the same rate as player maus, so once they get 500 EXP, they advance to Level 2.
Not receiving a share of treasure may cause resentment amongst hirelings.

Warbands

A maus is very small, and many beasts cannot be de- feated working alone. When maus work together, they can form a warband, and act on a scale capable of facing other warbands or large beasts, like a cat or boar.

Forming A Warband

A warband is formed by 20 or more fighting maus, plus one follower (luggage porter, cook, armourer) for every fighter.
Your maus can form a warband under their command. In a town or city, spend a week recruiting. Make a WIL save or pay 1000p.
Warbands start with:
d6 hp, STR 10, DEX 10, WIL 10
Damage: d6 clubs and axes
Warbands require 1000p a week for upkeep and wages. Failure to pay may result in mutiny.

Equipping

Equipment improves a warband’s abilities the same way as it does for individuals. Just multiply the purchase price by 20.

Battles

Battles between warbands and warband-scale creatures function the same as fights between small individuals. Attacks deal damage to HP, then to STR.
Attacks by a warband against a non-warband scale creature are enhanced, any damage taken from non-warband scale creatures is ignored unless it is particularly destructive or large scale.

Casualties

When a warband takes critical damage, they are broken and cannot act until rallied. At STR 0 the warband is wiped out. At half STR, the warband must make a WIL save or be routed.
Warbands recover HP and attribute score damage like individuals do, with Short, Long and Full rests.

Owning Property Owning property happens when you are given it, or you have taken it. In both cases, you may have to defend the property (especially if you stole it from someone who still wants it...) Either way, owning land allows you benefits in the town or area you own it.

Room and Board: Instead of staying in an inn overnight, you can sleep in your own room for free! Each room you own can house one player/hireling.

Storage:Storing your items in the bank is the safest place to store them. There is no chance for stealing or damaging them. (The only time they are taken or inaccessible is when the town is taken over by Rats or another malicious species). You can also store your items in a room you own for free! Each room that you own can store six (6) slots. This is the same amount in your pack. The quality of your room will dictate what happens to your room after 3 days of time passes.

Perks:Owning a home comes with perks. You become a citizen of the town and may get invited to parties and gatherings. Additionally, you may have access to certain people that you otherwise wouldn't have, such as powerful noblemaus, knights, or tradesmaus. Creatures come to your home and may ask for help or might have jobs and quests for you.
Tunnels aren't affected by quality. For aesthetic purposes, they look like the average of the two rooms they connect.

Poor Room

Poor rooms are barely suitable to live in. They contain crude ground, walls, and ceilings that are typically untreated whatsoever. It is not a comfortable place and probably smells. These rooms have the simplest of furniture such as a stool, table, and rickety beds. If it were in a fallen log, this room would be soggy and have splinters. It wouldn't have uniform walls and it might have holes in it where small bugs get through. The door would be a leaf or a small tapestry.

Standard Room

Standard rooms are cozy, comfortable, and quaint. They are hewn, sculpted, or otherwise shaped with tools to some degree. They have mostly symmetrical walls and sometimes include a simple pattern, wallpaper, paint, or fabric. They feel familiar, homey, and usually include a smell that gets familiar and comforting over your time spent there. They include a variety of furniture including dressers, couches, end tables, food storage cabinets, artwork, coat racks, and other typical items found in a home. If it were in a fallen log, this room would be carved and have flat and dry floors, walls, and ceiling. There would be a nice trim carved into the walls with a couple small nails for hanging drawings and coats. It would smell pleasant and may include a simple rug. The door would be a series of boards held together by string, or a wooden round with a hinge. May have a window with blinds.

Standard Room Perks: If you own a home with at least 2 rooms that are on average Standard quality, the following perks apply :

  1. You immediately gain one friend in town. Roll on the backgrounds and name table to find out who this friend is. This friend will give you details about the city, rumors they hear, and may even accompany you on some missions.
  2. You are invited to parties! Every once and a while, you'll get an invite to a party, wedding, celebration that includes food and some party favors like clothing, tools, weapons, or other items.
  3. People know you in town and you generally have a good reputation. When looking for hirelings, roll the die on the hireling chart twice and take the higher one.
  4. Neighbors will sometimes drop off food that they found or grew.

    5. Grand Room

    Grand rooms are exquisite and ornate. These rooms are built with precision and are sturdy. They often include beautiful rugs, artwork, and furniture and are simply pleasant to be in. Walls and furniture have intricate designs and everything is especially comfortable. The space smells fantastic and inhabitants feel a deep sense of security. They include special furniture like pianos, grand wardrobes, chandeliers, canopy bed frames, mirrors, and vanities. If it were in a fallen log, the wood inside would be stained and have a beautiful sheen. The ceilings and floors against the walls have intricately carved designs of animals, plants, or otherwise beautiful shapes. A grand rug covers the floor and detailed paintings hang on the walls. The room smells like incense and the finest of wood. The door would shut flush with the wall and be made of the same material. A brass knocker would be fixed to the front. Glass windows with curtains are next to the door.

    Grand Room Perks: If you own a home with at least 3 rooms that are on average Grand quality, you gain the perks of the Standard rooms and the following perks apply.

    1. You have access to nobles and leaders of the town you reside in if you didn't prior.
    2. Shop keepers will give you items for free in hopes you spend money there.
    3. You are invited to the finest of parties and are often given jewelry, artwork, or special gifts as party favors.
    4. While taking a Long Rest in the home, treat it like a Full Rest.
    5. After sleeping in the grand room, you are well-rested and have advantage on the first roll that day.

Saves

When you describe your maus doing something risky where the outcome is uncertain and failure has consequences, the GM will ask you to make a save against either STR, DEX or WIL.
To make a save, roll a d20. If the result is less than or equal to the relevant attribute, your maus succeeds, and suffers no consequences. If the result is over the attribute, your maus fails, and suffers the consequenc- es described by the GM.
• STR Saves: avoid harm through physical power and endurance
• DEX Saves: avoid harm through quick reaction, speed and agility
• WIL Saves: avoid harm through strength of will and force of personality

Opposed Saves

If your maus and another character are competing, you both make saves. The lowest successful roll wins.

Advantage and Disadvantage

If you roll a save and are particularly well prepared or equipped, the GM may allow you to roll with Advantage. Roll 2d20 and take the lowest result.
The GM may ask you to roll with Disadvantage if you are in bad position or poorly equipped for your current task. Roll 2d20 and take the highest result.

Combat

Combat is dangerous business for maus, even brave adventurers. But sometimes it cannot be avoided.
If you attack unsuspecting foes, your maus and any allies aware of the plan go first. Otherwise, make a DEX save to act before your opponents. In following combat rounds, maintain this order.
On your turn in a combat Round, your maus can move up to 12" and perform an action. Actions can be anything from negotiating, attacking, fleeing or performing a risky gambit (like tripping or disarming an opponent).

Attacks

Attacks always hit. Roll your weapon’s die and do that much damage to an opponent, minus their armor.
When an attack is impaired, such as when firing into cover, or fighting while grappled, roll d4 for damage regardless of weapon. When an attack is enhanced by a gambit or vulnerable opponent, roll d12.

Hit Protection and Damage

Damage is dealt first to a creature’s Hit Protection (HP). This represents the creature’s ability to avoid or shrug off real damage.
Once HP is depleted, damage is dealt to STR.
After taking STR damage, the creature must make a STR save. If they succeed, they are still able to fight. If they fail, they take critical damage.
When a creature takes critical damage, they take an Injured Condition, and are incapacitated until tended to by an ally and take a short rest.
If an incapacitated creature is left untended for 6 exploration Turns, they die.

Attribute Score Loss and Death

If a creature has their STR reduced to zero, they are dead. If DEX is reduced to zero, they are unable to move. If WIL is reduced to zero, they are reduced to insensibility.

Rest and Healing

There are three ways your maus can rest and recover.
• Short rest: takes 1 Turn. A swig of water and a few minutes of rest will restore d6+1 HP.
• Long rest: takes 1 Watch. A meal and some sleep will restore all HP. If HP was already full, restore d6 to an attribute score.
• Full rest: takes a week back in safety. This fully restores your maus’s ability scores and removes most long-term Conditions. A week of food and board in a settlement usually costs 20p.

Magic Spells

Spells are living spirits trapped by runes carved on obsidian tablets. Spells are usually found in deep and dangerous places. The creation of spells is an art lost to all but the most learned wizards, and they guard their secrets closely.

Selling spells

If unwanted, a fully charged spell can usually be sold for d6 x 100p in a settlement. A depleted spell’s value is halved.

Casting a Spell

To cast a spell, your maus must hold the tablet in a paw and read it aloud.
When your maus casts a spell, decide on the power to cast it with, up to the number of usage dots remaining on the spell.
Roll a number of d6 equal to the power the spell is cast with. For each die which shows the value 4, 5 or 6, mark one usage on the spell.
The spell has an effect, which varies depending on the number of [DICE] invested, and the [SUM] of the rolled dice.

Recharging A Spell

When a spell’s usage dots are filled, it is depleted and cannot be cast. Each spell has a recharge requirement. Fulfilling this requirement will coax the spirit back to the spell and clear all of its usage dots.

Upcast & Miscasts

Whenever one or more 1s are rolled when casting a spell, the caster increases the [DICE] by 1 for each 1 rolled. Then make a WIL save. If passed, take the Lucky Condition.
Whenever one or more 6s are rolled when casting a spell, the caster takes d6 damage to WIL for each 6 rolled. Then make a WIL save. If failed, take the Drained Condition.
1's and 6's cancel each other out.

Attributes

STR: physical strength and resilience.
DEX: speed and agility.
WIL: strength of will and charisma.

HP, Pips and Background

Hit Protection (HP): This is the damage your maus can shrug off before taking serious harm.
Pips: These are the basic currency of the maus kingdom.
Background: This is what your maus was before becoming an adventurer.

Inventory

Your maus carries items in their inventory slots. Most items take up one inventory slot. Some larger items, such as two-handed weapons and armor take up two slots.
Paw slots Items in these slots are carried.
Body slots Worn items can be swapped with paw slots as a free action.
Pack slots Items in these slots take time to retrieve when under pressure. While in combat, your maus must use an action (instead of attacking) to find an item in their pack.

Conditions

Conditions are negative effects suffered by your maus.
Each Condition must be placed in an inventory slot. maus may have mul- tiple copies of the same condition.
Some Conditions have additional effects, which apply as long as the Condition remains in your inventory.
Conditions can only be removed by meeting their clear requirement — usually a short, long or full rest.

Usage

Most items have three usage dots. When all three dots are marked on an item it is depleted or destroyed. Usage dots can be cleared from weapons/armor for 10% of the original cost per dot cleared.
• Weapons/armor/ammunition: after a fight, roll d6 for each item that was used during the fight. On 4-6, mark usage.
• Torches/lanterns: mark usage every 6 Turns.
• Rations: mark usage after a meal.
• Other gear: if used in a way that could break or deplete it, the GM may ask you to mark usage.

Banking

In a maus settlement, pips and items can be stored in a bank or safebox.
Your maus must pay a fee of 1% of the value when retrieving the stored pips or items.

Grit

Starting at second Level, your maus has Grit. This allows your maus to ignore Conditions.
For each point of Grit you have, you may place one Condition into the Grit space on your character sheet. Once placed into the Grit space, a Condition cannot be removed until cleared.

Food

To keep your mouse well, you need to eat food. Eating food with every rest prevents you from having the hungry condition. Rations easily do the trick and can be foraged for on the path. Artisan food or especially exquisite food is better than mere rations and can give you benefits besides satiating your hunger.

Meaty/Protein-rich foods: After eating, the next STR save you roll has advantage. Examples include: Fried crickets, chicken eggs, fish eggs, fish, lizard steaks

Sugary/fruity foods: After eating, the next DEX save you roll has advantage. Examples include: strawberry shortcake, pumpkin pie, ice cream, mom's cupcake, raisin cookies

Wholesome/vegetable-rich: After eating, the next WIL save you roll has advantage. Examples include: kale soup, potato stew, fajitas, broccoli w/ cheese, spicy red pepper chips, alote, sautéed Brussel sprouts

Chocolate: After eating, the next SAVE you roll has advantage.

Time

For convenience and ease of reference, game time is divided up into three time scales of expanding duration.
• Round: the timescale used during combat. Each Round is less than a minute.
• Turn: the timescale used during adventure site exploration. Each Turn is around 10 minutes, and is enough time to explore one room or perform an ac- tion or two. A fight will almost always last one Turn.
• Watch: the timescale used during wilderness travel. Each Watch is 36 Turns, about 6 hours. There are 4 watches in a day. You can usually travel 1 hex or mile in a watch.

Overland Travel

Your party of maus can travel one hex or mile per Watch. Difficult terrain (streams, human roads, large rocks, hills, etc.) require two Watches per mile traveled.

Foraging

In the wild, your maus can spend a Watch foraging. If they do, they will find d3 uses of rations.

Advancement

Your maus earns Experience Points (EXP) by bringing treasure and useful goods back from places of danger to the safety of a maus settlement.
For every pip-worth of treasure brought to safety (divid- ed equally amongst the party), your maus earns 1 EXP.
Your maus can earn additional EXP by spending their pips selflessly on improvements for the whole community. For every 10 pips spent this way, your maus earns 1 EXP.
